Executive Summary

The Gap: No Systematic Tracking Infrastructure

We can identify WHICH papers cite unconstrained mental rotation constructs (8,600+ papers from OpenAlex), but not HOW MUCH NSF funding supported this research. Current estimates ($200M-$750M) are based on assumed average costs per paper.

Critical Infrastructure Failure:

There is no systematic way to track which publications resulted from which grants. Papers should acknowledge NSF awards, but enforcement is inconsistent. NSF-PAR (Public Access Repository) exists, but compliance is imperfect. The mental rotation case reveals we can identify thousands of contaminated papers but cannot easily determine taxpayer investment or notify affected researchers.

NSF Data: What Exists vs. What We Need

✓ Currently Available

  • NSF Award Search - All awards since 1989 (web + API)
  • Award ID, PI, institution, amount, abstract, dates
  • NSF-PAR - Some publications→awards (2016+)
  • Public access, no special permission needed

✗ What's Missing

  • Comprehensive linkage - Publications→Awards for all time periods
  • Historical data (pre-2016 publication tracking)
  • Bulk export (API has rate limits)
  • Institutional compliance reports (internal NSF systems)

Our Matching Strategy:

  • PI Name Matching: Match publication authors to NSF PI names + award dates
  • Abstract Search: Find grants mentioning "mental rotation" in abstracts
  • Acknowledgment Parsing: Extract NSF award numbers from paper acknowledgments
  • Cross-validation: Combine all three methods for comprehensive coverage

Estimated coverage: 60-80% of NSF-funded papers with available data

Expected Outputs

  • Precise financial impact: Exact NSF investment in contaminated research
  • Field breakdown: Which directorates/programs funded affected studies
  • Temporal trends: When did funding peak? Is it still being awarded?
  • Institutional patterns: University-by-university accountability
  • Contamination severity: HIGH (primary construct) vs. MODERATE (background use)

Key Finding: Gender Balance Varies by Research Focus

The Vandenberg & Kuse (1978) citation network shows nearly equal male/female first authorship (42% vs. 40.5%), significantly more balanced than Shepard & Metzler citations (56.5% vs. 26.9%). This pattern reflects the role of the Mental Rotation Test in gender differences research and STEM ability assessment—a field that has attracted more female researchers studying spatial cognition.
